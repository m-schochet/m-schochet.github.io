Stellar Rotation Deep Learning

Schochet, M., Planet, P., Claytor, Z. R., Tayar, J., Feinstein, A. (in prep.), Stellar Rotation Period Prediction with Deep Learning from All Sky Automated Survey for Supernova Light Curves

Background

Stars form when their birth molecular cloud begins to collapse under self-gravitation, and the gas contracts ever more tightly forming a new star. That initial collapse is expected to conserve angular momentum, necessarily leading to stars being born rotation. Measuring that rotation is critical to understanding a star's history and its future evolution for a multitude of reasons. For one, it is empirically known that low-mass stars lose angular momentum (J) over their lifetimes, with a scaling relation where

J=t^(-0.5)

(see Skumanich 1972). This means that if we know the specific rotation rate of a given low-mass star, we can use it to understand that star's age.

Background In our forthcoming work, we attempt to predict stellar rotation periods on light curves from the All Sky Automated Survey for Supernova using a convolutional neural network that has previously been shown to accurately predict rotation periods for thousands of stars from the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (see Claytor et al. 2024). Once finished, all of our work will be available on Zenodo
