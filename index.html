Background

Stars form when their birth molecular cloud begins to collapse under self-gravitation, and the gas contracts ever more tightly forming a new star. That initial collapse is expected to conserve angular momentum, necessarily leading to stars being born rotation. Measuring that rotation is critical to understanding a star's history and its future evolution for a multitude of reasons. For one, it is empirically known that low-mass stars lose angular momentum (J) over their lifetimes, with a scaling relation where J=t^(-0.5) (see Skumanich 1972). This means that if we know the specific rotation rate of a given low-mass star, we can use it to understand that star's age.
